Seasonal Snow Cycle

How snow variables change through the hydrological year (September 1 – August 31) across elevation zones in Central Asia’s major river basins.

Reading the plot
  • Solid lines = historical baseline (1981–2010)
  • Dashed lines = near-future projection (2021–2040)
  • Dotted lines = far-future projection (2081–2100)
  • Colours = elevation bands from 0 m (purple) to 6000 m (yellow)

Projections are based on the TopoCLIM model chain (Fiddes et al., 2022) using bias-corrected CMIP6 climate scenarios and the FSM snow model. The gap between solid, dashed, and dotted lines reveals the projected near-term and long-term climate impact at each elevation.

Catchment Anomaly Map

Projected change in snow and climate variables for 295 river catchments across High Mountain Central Asia. Each catchment is coloured by its mean anomaly: the difference between the far-future period (2081–2100) and the historical baseline (1981–2010).

Variables
  • SWE – snow water equivalent (mm). Negative = less water stored in snowpack
  • Snow Depth – physical depth (m). Negative = shallower snowpack
  • Runoff – snowmelt runoff (mm). Changes affect downstream water supply timing
  • GST – ground surface temperature (°C). Always positive = warming across all catchments
  • Albedo – surface reflectivity. Negative = darker surface due to less snow cover

Hover over a catchment to see its mean anomaly value. Higher SSP scenarios (SSP5-8.5) represent stronger warming and generally show larger anomalies, particularly at lower elevations where snow cover is most vulnerable.

Methodology
1TopoSUB — Topographical Clustering

The landscape is partitioned into clusters of similar topographic and land-cover characteristics using the TopoSUB scheme. This reduces the computational domain from millions of grid cells to a tractable number of representative points, enabling high-resolution process-based modelling across Central Asia.

2TopoSCALE — Downscaling Current Climate

ERA5 reanalysis fields are topographically downscaled to each cluster centroid using TopoSCALE. This accounts for elevation-dependent lapse rates, terrain shading, and other sub-grid effects to produce realistic meteorological forcing at the point scale.

3TopoCLIM — Bias Correction of Future Scenarios

CMIP6 climate model outputs are bias-corrected and downscaled using quantile-delta mapping (TopoCLIM), ensuring that projected changes are anchored to the observed climate while preserving the climate change signal from the GCMs.

4FSM Snow Modelling

The Flexible Snow Model (FSM2) is driven with the downscaled forcing to simulate snowpack evolution, including snow accumulation, metamorphism, and melt. This produces spatially distributed fields of snow water equivalent, snow depth, runoff, albedo, and surface temperature.
